1. Measurement of Angle

Mathematics is the language of physics. Good knowledge of mathematics makes describing and applying physical principles much easier.

Arc Length Formula

$$\theta = \frac{\text{Arc Length}}{\text{Radius}} = \frac{l}{R}$$

$\theta$ MUST be in Radians.

Relationship Between Degrees and Radians

  • Degrees to Radians: Multiply by $\frac{\pi}{180}$
  • Radians to Degrees: Multiply by $\frac{180}{\pi}$
  • $1 \text{ Radian} \approx 57.3^{\circ}$
Degrees $30^{\circ}$ $45^{\circ}$ $60^{\circ}$ $90^{\circ}$ $120^{\circ}$ $135^{\circ}$ $270^{\circ}$ $360^{\circ}$
Radians $\frac{\pi}{6}$ $\frac{\pi}{4}$ $\frac{\pi}{3}$ $\frac{\pi}{2}$ $\frac{2\pi}{3}$ $\frac{3\pi}{4}$ $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ $2\pi$

2. Sign Convention for Angles

In physics and mathematics, the direction in which you measure an angle dictates its sign:

  • Anticlockwise Measurement: Positive ($+ve$) Angle.
  • Clockwise Measurement: Negative ($-ve$) Angle.

3. Trigonometric Functions & Limits

Using a right-angled triangle with Perpendicular ($P$), Base ($B$), and Hypotenuse ($H$):

$$\sin \theta = \frac{P}{H}$$
$$\csc \theta = \frac{H}{P}$$
$$\cos \theta = \frac{B}{H}$$
$$\sec \theta = \frac{H}{B}$$
$$\tan \theta = \frac{P}{B}$$
$$\cot \theta = \frac{B}{P}$$

Range of Trig Functions

Because the Hypotenuse is always the longest side, $P \le H$ and $B \le H$. Therefore:

$$-1 \le \sin \theta \le 1$$
$$-1 \le \cos \theta \le 1$$

However, $\tan\theta$ can take ANY real value: $-\infty < \tan\theta < \infty$.

5. Rules For Angles > 90° (CAST Rule)

Step 1: Identify the quadrant the angle lies in.
Step 2: Break the angle horizontally, i.e., use $(n\pi \pm \theta)$ or $(180^{\circ} \pm \theta)$, $(360^{\circ} \pm \theta)$.

Master Tip: Always break angles using horizontal lines ($180^{\circ}$ or $360^{\circ}$) because the trigonometric function does not change (e.g., $\sin$ remains $\sin$). Breaking vertically ($90^\circ$ or $270^\circ$) forces $\sin \leftrightarrow \cos$.

Example: $\sin(120^{\circ}) = \sin(180^{\circ} - 60^{\circ})$. Since $120^{\circ}$ is in the 2nd Quadrant, Sine is positive. So, $+ \sin(60^{\circ}) = +\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.

6. General Trigonometric Formulas

Fundamental Identities

$$\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$$
$$1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$$
$$1 + \cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta$$

Addition Formulas

$$\sin(A \pm B) = \sin A \cos B \pm \cos A \sin B$$
$$\cos(A \pm B) = \cos A \cos B \mp \sin A \sin B$$
$$\tan(A \pm B) = \frac{\tan A \pm \tan B}{1 \mp \tan A \tan B}$$

Double Angle Formulas (Very Important for Projectile Motion)

$$\sin 2\theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta$$
$$\cos 2\theta = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta$$
$$= 2\cos^2\theta - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta$$
$$\cos^2\theta = \frac{1 + \cos 2\theta}{2}$$
$$\sin^2\theta = \frac{1 - \cos 2\theta}{2}$$

7. Small Angle Approximations

For very small angles (generally when $\theta < 10^{\circ}$), we can simplify trigonometric calculations tremendously. Crucial Warning: $\theta$ must be converted into Radians for these approximations!

$$\sin \theta \approx \theta$$
$$\cos \theta \approx 1$$
$$\tan \theta \approx \theta$$

Example calculation: Find $\sin(2^{\circ})$

  • First, convert to radians: $2^{\circ} = 2 \times \frac{\pi}{180}$
  • Apply approximation: $\sin(2^{\circ}) \approx 2 \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{\pi}{90}$

8. Binomial Approximation

Very useful in Physics! When $x$ is very small compared to $1$ (Generally $x \ll 1$ or $x < 0.05$), higher powers of $x$ ($x^2, x^3$, etc.) become negligibly small.

General Formula:

$$(1 + x)^n \approx 1 + nx$$

Variations:

$$(1 - x)^n \approx 1 - nx$$
$$(1 + x)^{-n} \approx 1 - nx$$
$$(1 - x)^{-n} \approx 1 + nx$$

Physics Example: Gravity Variation with Height

Formula for gravity at height $h$: $$g_h = \frac{GM_e}{(R_e + h)^2} = g_s \left(1 + \frac{h}{R_e}\right)^{-2}$$

If height is very small compared to Earth's radius ($h \ll R_e$), applying binomial approximation:

$$g_h \approx g_s \left(1 - \frac{2h}{R_e}\right)$$

9. Progressions (A.P. & G.P.)

Arithmetic Progression (A.P)

Sequence: $a,\ a+d,\ a+2d,\ a+3d \dots$

$n^{th}$ Term:
$$T_n = a + (n-1)d$$

Sum of $n$ terms ($S_n$):
$$S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a + (n-1)d]$$
$$S_n = \frac{n}{2}[\text{First Term} + \text{Last Term}]$$

Geometric Progression (G.P)

Sequence: $a,\ ar,\ ar^2,\ ar^3 \dots$

$n^{th}$ Term:
$$T_n = a r^{n-1}$$

Sum of $n$ terms ($S_n$):
$$S_n = \frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$$

Sum of $\infty$ terms ($|r| < 1$):
$$S_\infty = \frac{a}{1-r}$$

Physics Example: Bouncing Ball (Infinite G.P)

A ball dropped from height $h$ bounces with decreasing velocities $v$, $ve$, $ve^2$, $ve^3 \dots$ (where $e < 1$ is the coefficient of restitution). Finding total distance or time requires an infinite G.P sum. Watch the simulation below.

Summing velocities: $$v + ve + ve^2 + \dots \infty = \frac{v}{1-e}$$

10. Important Series Expansions

Memorize these standard summations and series for complex physics derivations:

Sum of first $n$ natural numbers

$$1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$$

Sum of squares

$$1^2 + 2^2 + \dots + n^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$$

11. Coordinate Geometry (Lines, Circles & Parabolas)

Mastering coordinate geometry is absolutely essential for Kinematics Graphs (x-t, v-t, a-t).

1. The Straight Line

General Equation

$$y = mx + c$$

$m =$ slope $= \tan \theta$
$c =$ y-intercept

Equation from 2 points $(x_1, y_1)$ & $(x_2, y_2)$

$$(y - y_1) = m(x - x_1)$$

where $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$

Equation: y = 1x + 0

Slope Interpretations

  • If $\theta = 0^{\circ}$, then $m = 0$ (Horizontal Line).
  • If $\theta < 90^{\circ}$, then $m$ is positive (Line goes Up).
  • If $\theta = 90^{\circ}$, then $m = \infty$ (Vertical Line).
  • If $\theta > 90^{\circ}$, then $m$ is negative (Line goes Down).

2. The Parabola (Critical for Projectile Motion)

A curve where any point is at an equal distance from a fixed point (focus) and a fixed straight line (directrix). In physics, $y \propto x^2$ or $x \propto y^2$ always yields a parabola.

Symmetric about Y-axis

$$y = kx^2 \text{ or } x^2 = 4ay$$

Opens Upwards ($+k$) or Downwards ($-k$)

Symmetric about X-axis

$$x = ky^2 \text{ or } y^2 = 4ax$$

Opens Right ($+k$) or Left ($-k$)

3. The Equation of a Circle

For a circle with center at $(0,0)$ and radius $R$:

$$x^2 + y^2 = R^2$$

If the center is at $(h, k)$, the equation is: $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = R^2$

12. Concept of Differentiation

Differentiation is all about finding the instantaneous rate of change (or the exact slope at a single point on a curve).

  • $\Delta x$ (Finite Difference): A measurable, macroscopic change ($x_2 - x_1$).
  • $dx$ (Infinitesimal Difference): An extremely tiny, practically zero change in $x$.

Interactive Limit Engine: Secant to Tangent

Slide $\Delta x$ towards 0 to watch the average slope (Secant) morph perfectly into the instantaneous slope (Tangent).

Limit Definition: As the interval $\Delta x$ approaches zero, the average slope becomes the instantaneous slope.

$$f'(x) = \frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \tan \theta$$

13. Master Rules of Differentiation

Basic Rules

Constant Rule

$$\frac{d}{dx}(c) = 0$$

Power Rule

$$\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = n x^{n-1}$$

Constant Multiple

$$\frac{d}{dx}(c u) = c \frac{du}{dx}$$

Algebraic Rules

Sum/Difference Rule

$$\frac{d}{dx}(u \pm v) = \frac{du}{dx} \pm \frac{dv}{dx}$$

Product Rule

$$\frac{d}{dx}(uv) = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}$$

Quotient Rule

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{u}{v}\right) = \frac{v\frac{du}{dx} - u\frac{dv}{dx}}{v^2}$$

Trigonometric & Exponential Rules

Function $f(x)$ Derivative $f'(x)$ Function $f(x)$ Derivative $f'(x)$
$\sin x$ $\cos x$ $\tan x$ $\sec^2 x$
$\cos x$ $-\sin x$ $\cot x$ $-\csc^2 x$
$e^x$ $e^x$ $\sec x$ $\sec x \tan x$
$\ln x$ $\frac{1}{x}$ $\csc x$ $-\csc x \cot x$

14. Chain Rule & Double Differentiation

Chain Rule: Used when dealing with a "function inside a function". Differentiate the outer function first, then multiply by the derivative of the inner function.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{dt} \times \frac{dt}{dx}$$

Example: $\frac{d}{dt}(\sin(\omega t)) = \cos(\omega t) \times \frac{d}{dt}(\omega t) = \omega \cos(\omega t)$

Double Differentiation in Physics (Kinematics)

Differentiating a function twice is denoted by $f''(x)$ or $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$. In physics, it flawlessly links position, velocity, and acceleration.

Velocity ($v$)

$$v = \frac{ds}{dt}$$

Acceleration ($a$)

$$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2}$$

Also: $a = v \frac{dv}{ds}$

15. Maxima and Minima

To find the absolute highest (maxima) or lowest (minima) point of a function, we look for places where the tangent slope is exactly zero.

Local Maxima

1. Slope is zero: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$

2. Rate of slope change:
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0$ (Negative)

Local Minima

1. Slope is zero: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$

2. Rate of slope change:
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0$ (Positive)

If differentiation is about finding the rate of change (slope), integration is about summing up tiny slices to find the total amount. Geometrically, Integration represents the Area under the Curve.

Physics Interpretations

  • If $v = \frac{dx}{dt}$, then displacement $x = \int v \cdot dt$ (Area under $v-t$ graph).
  • If $W = F \cdot dx$, then total Work $W = \int F \cdot dx$ (Area under $F-x$ graph).

Core Integration Formulas

Don't forget the constant of integration ($+C$) for indefinite integrals!

Power Rule

$$\int x^n \, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$$

(Valid for $n \neq -1$)

Special Case ($n = -1$)

$$\int \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \ln |x| + C$$
Integral Result Integral Result
$\int \sin x \, dx$ $-\cos x + C$ $\int \sec^2 x \, dx$ $\tan x + C$
$\int \cos x \, dx$ $\sin x + C$ $\int e^x \, dx$ $e^x + C$

Definite Integrals

When boundaries ($a$ to $b$) are provided, evaluate the integral and apply the upper limit minus the lower limit. No $+C$ is needed.

$$\int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = [F(x)]_{a}^{b} = F(b) - F(a)$$

17. Introduction to Vectors

Physical quantities are primarily divided into two categories:

  • Scalars: Require only magnitude to describe completely (e.g., Mass, Distance, Speed, Work). They obey simple algebraic addition.
  • Vectors: Require BOTH magnitude and a specific direction, AND must obey the laws of vector addition (e.g., Displacement, Velocity, Force, Acceleration).

18. Addition of Vectors (Parallelogram Law)

If two vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ are placed tail-to-tail at an angle $\theta$, their resultant $\vec{R}$ is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by them.

Interactive Vector Addition

Adjust the angle $\theta$ between vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ to see how their Resultant $\vec{R}$ changes dynamically.

Resultant |R|: 15.62

Resultant Magnitude $|R|$

$$|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{A^2 + B^2 + 2AB\cos\theta}$$

Resultant Direction ($\alpha$ with $\vec{A}$)

$$\tan\alpha = \frac{B\sin\theta}{A + B\cos\theta}$$

Limits of Resultant: The resultant magnitude always lies between the sum and difference of the two vectors:
$$|A - B| \le |\vec{R}| \le A + B$$

Max when $\theta = 0^\circ$ (Parallel), Min when $\theta = 180^\circ$ (Anti-parallel).

19. Resolution of Vectors (Critical for NLM)

We can break any vector $\vec{R}$ into two perpendicular components along the X and Y axes. This process is called "Resolution".

Horizontal Component

$$R_x = R \cos \theta$$

Vertical Component

$$R_y = R \sin \theta$$

Once resolved, the vector can be written mathematically in unit vector notation: $\vec{R} = R_x \hat{i} + R_y \hat{j}$

Finding Vector from Components (2D)

Magnitude

$$|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}$$

Angle with X-axis ($\theta$)

$$\tan \theta = \frac{R_y}{R_x}$$

3D Vector Components & Direction Cosines

For a vector in 3D space: $\vec{R} = R_x \hat{i} + R_y \hat{j} + R_z \hat{k}$

3D Magnitude

$$|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2 + R_z^2}$$

Direction Cosines ($\alpha, \beta, \gamma$)

$$\cos \alpha = \frac{R_x}{|\vec{R}|}, \quad \cos \beta = \frac{R_y}{|\vec{R}|}, \quad \cos \gamma = \frac{R_z}{|\vec{R}|}$$

Identity: $\cos^2\alpha + \cos^2\beta + \cos^2\gamma = 1$

20. Vector Multiplication

Vectors can be multiplied in two ways, producing either a scalar result or a vector result. This defines Work, Power, Flux, Torque, and Angular momentum in physics.

1. Dot Product (Scalar)

Yields a pure number. Measures how much vectors align.

$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = |\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\cos\theta$$

Physics Application:
Work Done $W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d}$

  • $\hat{i}\cdot\hat{i} = \hat{j}\cdot\hat{j} = \hat{k}\cdot\hat{k} = 1$
  • $\hat{i}\cdot\hat{j} = \hat{j}\cdot\hat{k} = \hat{k}\cdot\hat{i} = 0$

2. Cross Product (Vector)

Yields a new vector perpendicular to both $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$.

$$\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = |\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\sin\theta \, \hat{n}$$

Physics Application:
Torque $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$

  • $\hat{i}\times\hat{j} = \hat{k}$, $\hat{j}\times\hat{k} = \hat{i}$, $\hat{k}\times\hat{i} = \hat{j}$
  • $\hat{i}\times\hat{i} = 0$ (Parallel vectors cross = 0)
